Inference about Variance

Sometimes the consistency (variance) of a process is more important than the average. We use Chi-Square and F-distributions here.

Inference about a Single Population Variance ($\sigma^2$)

We use the Chi-Square ($\chi^2$) Distribution.

Test Statistic:

$$ \chi^2 = \frac{(n-1)s^2}{\sigma_0^2} $$

Where $s^2$ is sample variance, $\sigma_0^2$ is hypothesized variance, and $df = n-1$.

Quality Control

A machine fills bottles. We want variance $\sigma^2 \le 0.01$. We take 20 samples ($n=20$) and find $s^2 = 0.015$. Is the variance too high?

$$ \chi^2 = \frac{19 \times 0.015}{0.01} = 28.5 $$

Critical value for $\alpha=0.05, df=19$ is approx 30.14. Since $28.5 < 30.14$, we fail to reject $H_0$. The variance is acceptable.

Inference about Two Population Variances

We use the F-Distribution to compare two variances (e.g., is Process A more consistent than Process B?).

Test Statistic:

$$ F = \frac{s_1^2}{s_2^2} $$

Convention: Put the larger variance on top so $F \ge 1$.

Degrees of freedom: $df_1 = n_1 - 1$ (numerator), $df_2 = n_2 - 1$ (denominator).
